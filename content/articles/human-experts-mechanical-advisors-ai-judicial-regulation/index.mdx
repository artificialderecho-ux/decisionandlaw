## From Human Experts to Mechanical Advisors: A Regulatory Blueprint for AI in Courts Inspired by Judicial Expert Frameworks

The architecture of judicial decision-making is undergoing a tectonic transformation. For centuries, the judge has occupied the apex of epistemological authority in the courtroom, relying, in cases of technical complexity, on human experts: the *auxiliaires de justice*. However, in the last two years, this delegative structure has taken an algorithmic turn. It is no longer only flesh-and-blood experts explaining ballistics or forensic accounting; now artificial intelligence (AI) systems act as de facto advisors on the bench. In 2023, a judge in Cartagena, **Colombia**, made history by admitting the use of ChatGPT to resolve a dispute over the fundamental right to health of an autistic child, posing legal questions to the model to support his *ratio decidendi*. Barely a year later, in June 2024, a court in the **Netherlands** (Rechtbank Gelderland) used a large language model (LLM) to determine the average useful life of solar panels in a damages dispute, directly incorporating the technical data into its evidentiary assessment. In the **United States**, Judge Kevin Newsom of the Eleventh Circuit formally proposed in 2024 the use of LLMs to interpret contractual and legal terms, arguing that such models capture the "ordinary meaning" of language better than traditional dictionaries.

This integration is not merely a technological enhancement; it is a redefinition of the judicial function. While AI promises to mitigate court congestion and standardise criteria, its current deployment occurs in an operational vacuum that threatens the foundations of the rule of law. The most immediate risk is **technical opacity**. Many of these systems operate as "black boxes" whose internal processes are inscrutable even to their developers, making transparent judicial reasoning impossible. This lack of transparency fuels **automation bias**—the well-documented human tendency, from which judges are not exempt, to blindly trust computerised results—resulting in a **covert delegation** of judicial authority. When a judge adopts an algorithmic recommendation without the ability to audit its logic, the right to a fair trial and due process, enshrined in **Article 6 of the European Convention on Human Rights (ECHR)** , becomes an empty formality. Judicial independence is compromised not by political pressure but by epistemological submission to software.

The global regulatory response to these challenges has so far been disappointing due to its fragmentation and lack of enforceability. We are witnessing a proliferation of soft law instruments that, although well-intentioned, lack **enforceability**. Supranational institutions such as the **CEPEJ** and **UNESCO** have issued ethical charters and guidelines that underscore high-level principles such as transparency and human oversight. At the national level, countries such as the United Kingdom, Canada, New Zealand, Australia, Belgium, and France have published guidance for their judges. However, these guidelines are often vague; they exhort judges to "be cautious" or "verify results," but they do not offer binding technical standards or specific procedures for parties to challenge the tool. Even the recent EU **AI Act**, although classifying judicial AI as "high-risk," delegates much of the concrete implementation to future standards that have not yet materialised in the courtroom. The result is a regulatory landscape characterised by uncertainty, where the legality of AI use depends more on the individual judge's technological enthusiasm than on a robust normative framework.

This article argues that the solution to this algorithmic "Wild West" does not necessarily require inventing new legal categories from scratch. Instead, it advances a central thesis: **existing legal frameworks for judicial experts offer a superior functional and normative model for regulating AI in courts.** Human experts and AI systems perform the same epistemic function: they translate complex knowledge or massive volumes of data into information useful to the judge. If the law has already devised centuries-old safeguards to control human experts—ensuring their independence, limiting their mandate, and allowing parties to contest their reports—those same obligations can and must be transposed to "mechanical advisors." By treating AI not as a mere office tool but as a **de facto expert**, we can demand levels of accountability and auditability that abstract ethical principles fail to impose.

To develop this thesis, the article is structured in four main parts. **Part I** analyses the traditional legal framework of judicial experts in civil law and common law systems, identifying the obligations that guarantee the integrity of the process. **Part II** establishes the functional analogy between judicial AI and the human expert, demonstrating that both operate under a presumption of technical authority that the judge cannot validate alone. **Part III**, the propositional core of the work, details the transposition of specific obligations: from the creation of **national registers** of certified AI systems and the requirement of an **oath** of algorithmic fidelity, to the obligation to issue an **adversarial report** that allows parties to exercise real technical control. Finally, **Part IV** explores the limits of this analogy, acknowledging that AI lacks moral personality and analysing how to allocate **liability** for machine errors. The conclusion offers a roadmap for justice systems to move from passive adoption of AI to active governance based on transparency and accountability.

## Judicial Experts as a Regulatory Archetype: Duties, Independence and Procedural Safeguards

The regulation of expert evidence in contemporary legal systems is not merely a matter of evidentiary technique but a structural component of the right to effective judicial protection. The judicial expert acts as an epistemological bridge between the technical complexity of reality and the adjudicative function of the judge. In the civil law (continental) model, which serves as the basis for this study, the expert is not a party witness but an **auxiliary of justice** (*auxiliaire de justice*), a neutral figure whose mission is to assist the court in understanding facts that require specialised knowledge beyond the judge's ordinary competence. This section analyses the normative framework, deontological obligations, and procedural safeguards that make the human expert the ideal regulatory archetype for the future governance of judicial artificial intelligence.

### Definition and Functions: Neutrality as Cornerstone

A judicial expert is defined as an individual or entity possessing scientific, technical, artistic or specialised knowledge, formally appointed by the court to issue a professional opinion on factual questions. Unlike an ordinary witness, who testifies about facts perceived by the senses, the expert provides a **technical value judgment** based on experience and training. The expert's function is advisory, and although the conclusions carry significant authority, they are not binding on the judge, who retains ultimate discretion to assess their probative value according to the rules of sound reasoning.

It is imperative to distinguish the judicial expert from other procedural figures. Whereas a **party-appointed consultant** is hired by litigants to strengthen their argument, the judicial expert must act with strict **neutrality and impartiality**, owing ultimate loyalty to the court rather than to the party paying the fees. In common law systems, the prevailing model is the *expert witness*, where each party presents its own expert, often resulting in a "battle of experts." However, the continental trend—and that recommended by guides such as the **EGLE 2015**—emphasises court appointment to mitigate strategic bias and ensure that the evidence serves the higher interest of justice.

### Normative Sources and European Convergence

Notwithstanding Member States' procedural autonomy, there is notable normative convergence driven by institutions such as the **European Expertise and Expert Institute (EEEI)** and the **CEPEJ**. The civil procedure codes of France, Belgium, and the Netherlands provide robust frameworks that have inspired transnational standards. The fundamental instrument in this field is the **European Guide for Legal Expertise (EGLE 2015)** , which proposes a "jus commune" for expert evidence based on transparency, quality and integrity. In addition, CEPEJ evaluation reports underline the need to protect the expert title and standardise selection processes to strengthen public trust in the administration of justice.

### Essential Obligations of the Judicial Expert

The regulatory model for experts rests on a set of obligations that guarantee the legitimacy of their intervention:

- **Court appointment:** Expert intervention is not automatic. Under continental law, appointment must satisfy criteria of **utility** (capacity to resolve the dispute), **strict necessity** (where the judge lacks technical competence), and **proportionality** (in relation to cost and complexity). The court retains discretion to appoint the expert *ex officio* or at the parties' request, ensuring that the mandate is precise and does not encroach upon legal interpretation, which is the exclusive province of the judge.
- **National registers and accreditation:** To ensure quality, experts must be listed in **official registers** managed by ministries of justice or independent bodies. Entry requirements are rigorous: higher academic education, accredited professional experience (often a minimum of five years), proven knowledge of procedural rules, and unimpeachable moral integrity. These registers are not mere directories but quality-control mechanisms conferring a "protected title" on the expert.
- **Judicial oath:** Upon appointment or registration, the expert must take a **formal oath**. In Belgium, for example, the expert swears to carry out the assignment "in honour and conscience, with diligence and honesty." This act ritualises the expert's legal and moral responsibility, binding the findings to procedural truth.
- **Code of ethics:** The expert's conduct is governed by principles of **independence, impartiality, competence and confidentiality**. Loyalty to the court requires the expert to disclose any actual or potential conflict of interest that might cloud judgment. Moreover, the expert has a duty of **transparency and justification**, which requires thorough documentation of methods and sources, enabling both judge and parties to audit the internal logic.

### The Adversarial Principle and the Right to Challenge

Here lies the core mechanism protecting the due process rights enshrined in the ECHR. The **right to contest** expert evidence is not a mere procedural courtesy; it is a substantive guarantee of procedural fairness. Under Article 6 of the ECHR, enshrined in landmark decisions such as *Mantovanelli v. France* (1997), *Cottin v. Belgium* (2005), and *Martins Silva v. Portugal* (2014), the European Court of Human Rights (ECtHR) has consistently held that the right to a fair trial includes the right to effectively challenge evidence—particularly expert evidence—upon which the decision fundamentally depends.

The adversarial principle requires:

1. **Transparency of the expert report:** The report must be made available to all parties, not just the judge.
2. **Right to cross-examination:** Each party has the right to submit counter-expertise, challenge the methodology, and probe the expert's impartiality.
3. **Reasoned judgment:** The judge must address the expert's conclusions in the motivation of the judgment, explaining either why they were accepted or rejected.
4. **Accessibility of methodology:** The expert must disclose not only conclusions but the methods, assumptions, and limitations underlying them.

This procedural architecture is fundamentally incompatible with opaque, algorithmically-derived advice that neither the judge nor the parties can meaningfully audit. **The integration of AI into judicial reasoning without these safeguards violates international human rights standards.**

## The Functional Equivalence Thesis: Why AI Systems Are De Facto Experts

The argument for treating judicial AI as de facto experts rests on functional, not formal, equivalence. The critical insight is that **neither human experts nor AI systems possess inherent authority; both derive legitimacy exclusively from their ability to provide reliable specialized knowledge that the judge cannot independently verify.**

### The Epistemological Position of the Expert (Human or Machine)

A judge confronting a complex technical question (ballistics analysis, semiconductor defect analysis, medical causation) faces an epistemological asymmetry: the judge does not possess the knowledge to independently validate the answer. The judge must therefore rely on an external source—a human expert or an AI system—to translate technical complexity into legal relevance.

In both cases:

- The expert (human or machine) occupies a position of **informational authority** vis-à-vis the judge.
- The judge's decision-making authority is **contingent upon the expert's credibility.**
- **Automation bias** risks applying equally: the judge may defer to the expert not from reasoned assessment but from the presumption that the expert "must know."

The functional equivalence thesis contends that these structural similarities demand equivalent procedural protections. If the law requires a human expert to be neutral, competent, transparent, and challengeable, then **an AI system providing technical guidance in judicial proceedings must satisfy the same standards.**

### Disanalogy and Hierarchy

A critical question immediately arises: can an AI system be held to the same standards as a human expert? The answer is necessarily nuanced. AI systems lack **moral agency** and **legal personality**. They cannot be sanctioned for perjury, cannot violate professional ethics in a culpable sense, and cannot be held personally liable for negligence. 

However, **functional equivalence does not require perfect analogy.** Rather, it requires that the *protective mechanism* be adapted to the specific nature of the advisor. Where human experts are bound by oath and professional codes, AI systems must be bound by **technical auditability and structural safeguards.** Where human experts are held liable through professional misconduct proceedings, AI systems are regulated through **developer accountability and product liability frameworks.**

The functional equivalence thesis therefore translates into a hierarchy of obligations:

1. **Appointment criteria:** Both must be selected based on utility and necessity.
2. **Transparency and documentation:** Both must disclose their reasoning processes.
3. **Contestability:** Both must be subject to adversarial challenge.
4. **Accountability:** Both must have clear lines of responsibility when errors cause harm.

## Transposing Expert Obligations to AI Systems: A Regulatory Scheme

The normative contribution of this analysis lies in translating the procedural safeguards already embedded in expert witness regulation into a concrete regulatory scheme for judicial AI. This section details four pillars: **designation and necessity, registration and accreditation, transparency through RAG, and the right to adversarial contest.**

### Pillar 1: Strict Criteria for Appointment Based on Utility and Necessity

Judicial AI should not be used simply because it exists or because it promises efficiency. Courts should be required to satisfy three explicit criteria before deploying AI:

**Utility:** Does the AI system resolve the specific technical or analytical challenge at hand? A system trained on English case law may lack utility for comparative law analysis. A model with known biases in certain demographic classifications may lack utility in bail determination.

**Strict necessity:** Can the judge or the parties resolve the matter without AI? If human expertise is available, practical, and reliable, necessity is not satisfied. Only when volume, speed, or technical complexity genuinely exceed human capacity should AI deployment be justified.

**Proportionality:** Is the cost—in terms of opacity, potential error, and procedural complexity—proportional to the benefit? High-stakes decisions (liberty, custody, fundamental rights) require higher thresholds of necessity and lower tolerances for opacity.

**Procedural requirement:** Courts should be obliged to issue a **written justification** explaining why AI was deemed necessary. This justification should be disclosed to the parties before the AI system is consulted. Parties should have the right to challenge the AI's appointment on grounds that necessity or utility are unsatisfied.

### Pillar 2: National Registers of Certified AI Systems

Just as judicial experts must be accredited and listed in official registers, judicial AI systems should be subject to **certification and registration** at the national level. Each register would document:

- **System identifier:** Model name, version, developer, date of certification.
- **Scope of application:** Jurisdictional limits, case types for which the system is approved.
- **Accuracy and limitation data:** Documented performance metrics, known biases, failure modes.
- **Auditability features:** Whether the system can generate structured reports (see Pillar 3), document its sources, and explain its reasoning.
- **Periodic review:** Certification should lapse after a defined period (e.g., two years), requiring recertification and updated accuracy assessments.

**Responsibility:** National Ministries of Justice, in consultation with bar associations and academic experts, would establish certification criteria. Developers would submit systems for review; certification would not grant monopoly rights but would serve as a quality assurance mechanism.

### Pillar 3: Digital Code of Ethics and Auditability via RAG

Human experts are bound by ethical codes governing **independence, confidentiality, competence, and transparency.** For AI systems, direct ethical compliance is impossible (they lack moral agency), but the *underlying safeguards* can be operationalized through technical means.

The core technique is **Retrieval Augmented Generation (RAG).** Rather than treating an AI system as a black box that generates conclusions, RAG requires the system to:

1. **Identify the question or task** explicitly.
2. **Retrieve relevant sources** (case law, statutes, doctrinal authorities) used to formulate the response.
3. **Document the reasoning chain:** explicitly state the sources cited, the interpretations applied, and the logical steps leading to the conclusion.
4. **Generate a structured report,** not a narrative response.

**Operationally, this means:**

Courts would be required to demand from AI systems a standardized report format:

```
QUERY: [What the judge asked]
SOURCES RETRIEVED: [List of cases, statutes, or documents cited]
REASONING: [Step-by-step logical explanation]
CAVEATS AND LIMITATIONS: [Acknowledged uncertainties, known biases]
CONCLUSION: [The technical finding]
```

This transforms the AI from an oracle into a **witness-like figure** whose reasoning can be examined, contested, and either accepted or rejected by the judge with full transparency.

### Pillar 4: The Adversarial Pre-Report and the Right of Contestation

The most critical innovation is the **mandatory pre-report and adversarial comment phase** before AI-derived information becomes part of the judicial record.

**Procedure:**

1. The judge consults the AI system and receives its report (in the RAG format above).
2. Before relying on the report in the decision, the judge is required to **disclose the report to the parties** and provide a reasonable time window (e.g., 15 days) for comment.
3. Parties have the right to:
   - Critique the AI's sources or interpretation
   - Present contrary expert opinion
   - Challenge the accuracy of the data or the methodology
   - Request that the judge disregard the AI's output
4. The judge is required to **address the parties' contestations** in the judgment's reasoning, explaining why the AI's report was accepted, rejected, or qualified.

This mechanism operationalizes the **adversarial principle**—the core procedural guarantee under Article 6 ECHR—for AI-derived evidence. It prevents the covert delegation of judicial authority because the parties retain meaningful opportunity to challenge the AI's role.

## Liability and Accountability: The Distributed Responsibility Model

A fundamental question remains unanswered in current AI regulation: **who is responsible when an AI system errs?** The AI system itself has no legal personality, no assets, and cannot be sanctioned. Therefore, responsibility must be distributed among three actors: the **developer**, the **operator (judge/court)**, and the **state**.

### Developer Liability

Under the proposed **EU AI Liability Directive**, developers of high-risk AI systems (judicial AI is explicitly high-risk) are liable for **defects in design or information.** If an AI system was trained on biased data, if its architecture has known failure modes that result in discriminatory outputs, or if the developer failed to conduct adequate testing, the developer bears liability.

**Practically, this means:**

- Developers must conduct documented bias audits before deploying systems in judicial contexts.
- Developers must maintain accessible documentation of training data, model architecture, and known limitations.
- Developers are subject to product liability claims if their system causes demonstrable harm (e.g., an erroneous risk assessment leading to wrongful detention).

### Operator (Judicial) Liability

Courts and judges bear liability for **negligent or improper use** of AI systems. If a judge deployed an AI system without satisfying the necessity criteria, failed to disclose the AI's involvement to parties, or ignored clear caveats and limitations in the system's output, the judge (or the court system) bears responsibility.

**Practically, this means:**

- Courts must maintain records of which AI systems were consulted, when, and for what purpose.
- Judges must document their reasoning for choosing to rely (or not rely) on AI-derived information.
- Procedural rights of challenge (Pillar 4) are enforceable through appellate review; if the judge violated these rights, the decision may be overturned on appeal.

### State Responsibility

Finally, the state bears responsibility for **regulatory failure.** If a state permits AI deployment in courts without establishing the frameworks proposed above (registers, transparency, pre-reports, contestation rights), the state itself violates the rule of law and may incur liability under human rights law.

**Practically, this means:**

- States must establish the institutional infrastructure (registers, certification, disclosure requirements).
- States must ensure that procedures for challenging AI evidence are accessible and effective.
- International human rights bodies (ECtHR, UN bodies) can review whether a state's regulatory framework for judicial AI meets the standards of fair trial and due process.

## Limits of the Expert Analogy and Future Horizons

The expert framework offers a powerful regulatory template, but it has limits. AI systems are fundamentally different from human experts in ways that demand specialized legal attention.

**First, the question of legal personality:** A human expert is a rights-bearing person who can be criminally sanctioned, professionally disciplined, and held civilly liable. An AI system is property—software—and lacks legal personality. This means that while we can transpose *procedural* safeguards to AI (transparency, contestability), we cannot replicate *moral accountability*. The remedy, therefore, is to hold **developers and courts** accountable for the AI's behavior, not the AI itself.

**Second, the scale problem:** A single human expert might influence a hundred cases in a career. A language model might influence millions of judicial decisions within months. If the AI system has a systematic bias—undetected despite efforts to identify it—the liability exposure and systemic injustice could be far greater than any individual expert's misconduct. This suggests the need for **continuous post-deployment monitoring** and rapid procedures to suspend certified systems if evidence of systematic bias emerges.

**Third, the interpretability barrier:** Human experts can explain their reasoning in natural language, can be cross-examined in real time, and can clarify ambiguities. Large language models (LLMs) operate through opaque neural networks that even their designers do not fully understand. While RAG can document what sources the model retrieved and what output it generated, it cannot fully explain *why* the model made certain inferential choices. This gap between transparency and interpretability may require higher thresholds for AI deployment in cases involving fundamental rights or complex legal interpretation.

## Roadmap for Supranational Harmonization

The fragmentation of current approaches—ethical charters without enforceability, national guidelines without coordination, regulatory uncertainty—demands a coordinated response. This roadmap proposes three levels of action:

### National Level

Each state should establish:

1. **A national register** of certified AI systems for judicial use, with clear certification criteria and periodic review.
2. **Procedural rules** requiring disclosure of AI consultation to parties and providing a structured contestation phase.
3. **Training programs** for judges on AI literacy, including understanding the capabilities and limitations of specific systems.
4. **Appellate oversight** ensuring that lower courts comply with procedural safeguards and that AI-derived conclusions are not adopted without adequate justification.

### European/Regional Level

Supranational bodies such as the Council of Europe (through CEPEJ) and the European Union (through the European Commission) should:

1. **Harmonize minimum standards** for judicial AI certification, allowing for reciprocal recognition across jurisdictions.
2. **Establish a supranational registry** of certified systems, enabling smaller states or courts with limited resources to benefit from centralized vetting.
3. **Develop binding recommendations** (or, preferably, a directive-level legal instrument) requiring EU member states to implement the safeguards outlined above.
4. **Create advisory bodies** to continuously update standards as AI technology evolves and as empirical evidence of AI's impact on justice emerges.

### Global Level

International organizations such as the United Nations, UNESCO, and the International Commission of Jurists should:

1. **Develop model legislation** that states can adapt to their own legal traditions.
2. **Establish international standards** for AI literacy and training in the judicial profession.
3. **Create mechanisms** for sharing information about AI system failures and lessons learned across jurisdictions.

## Conclusion: From Passive Adoption to Active Governance

The integration of artificial intelligence into courtrooms is not a distant prospect—it is occurring now, in courts from Cartagena to The Hague to federal courts across the United States. The legal system can respond in one of two ways: passively, by treating AI as a tool beyond regulatory reach, accepting the erosion of transparency and fairness; or actively, by developing governance frameworks that maintain the rule of law while harnessing AI's efficiency benefits.

This article has argued for the second path. The regulatory solution does not require inventing entirely new legal categories or imposing burdensome restrictions on technological development. Instead, it requires recognizing that **AI systems used in judicial reasoning perform the same epistemological function as human experts** and are therefore subject to the same structural safeguards: independence through oversight, transparency through documentation, and contestability through adversarial procedures.

The framework of judicial experts—refined over centuries in continental law and increasingly recognized in common law systems—offers a proven template for these safeguards. By transposing obligations of registration, transparency, ethics, and contestation to AI systems, justice systems can ensure that algorithmic efficiency does not corrode the epistemological integrity of judicial proceedings or the fundamental rights of litigants to a fair trial.

The roadmap is clear: national certification registers, mandatory disclosure and contestation procedures, structured reports using techniques like RAG, and distributed liability among developers, courts, and states. These measures are technically feasible, legally coherent, and aligned with international human rights standards.

The alternative—continued regulatory drift, fragmented soft law, and individual judges' ad hoc decisions about when and how to deploy opaque AI systems—threatens the rule of law itself. The time for active governance is now.
